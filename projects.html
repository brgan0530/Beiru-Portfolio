3-Stage Current Starved Voltage-Controlled Oscillator

Project Description:

In this project, I worked in a team of 3 members to create a full swing 3-Stage Current Starved Voltage-Controlled Oscillator (VCO). I contributed by creating the schematics, simulations and results analysis for prelayout and postlayout.

Skills Covered:

Cadence Virtuoso (Schematics, ADE-L Simulations), Python for calculations, Excel for Data Visualisation, Documentation with Google Collab

Project Details:

Final Schematics of Current Starved VCO (VCO+DCC Block)

The VCO block schematics comprises of a 3-stage inverter chain which creates a ring oscillator. To ensure that a square clock is outputted we added a 2-stage inverter chain at the output of the ring oscillator to create a non-inverting buffer.

Final Schematics of VCO Block

A duty cycle corrector (DCC) was also further added to the VCO block by using a D Flip Flop in feedback (half frequency counter) to ensure that the square wave outputted achieves 50% duty cycle. DCC was added as we noticed variations in duty cycle as control voltage changes during prelayout simulations.

Final Schematics of DCC Block

Prelayout simulations were done to tune the cmos transitor sizing of buffer inverters (ring oscillator inverter specifications were fixed due to project requirements) and to check for performance of system. I used the parametric sweep function in ADE-L to improve the efficiency of the simulations.

Prelayout Test Bench of VCO+DCC

The DCC was eventually added in our final schematics as our prelayout simulation validated the effectiveness of the DCC. Our results showed that The duty cycle of the clock improved from max 6% deviation from 50% without DCC to max 3% deviation from 50% with DCC. However, the trade off is that clock frequency after DCC was halved. With DCC, frequency ranges from 16.2-412.3MHz. The data for the excel plot was obtained measurement we obtained from the waveforms generated using ADE-L.

Prelayout Simulation Excel Plots

After prelayout simulations, we moved onto layout. The total area of our layout including the DCC is 60.15 x 27.4 um, for an area of 1648.11 um^2. As my teammate was in charge of layout, I assisted him by suggesting ways which I think can help to reduce overall size such as the changing the orientation of current source and the introduction of metal interconnect 2 to reduce polygate length.

Final Layout

Extra Learning: I did a layout of VCO Block as I wanted to explore if overall size can be further reduced (assumed project specifications not fixed)

After final layout for VCO+DCC block, QRC extraction was done to evaluate effect of parasitics postlayout. Similarly, ADE-L simulation was done and functionality (frequency, duty cycle) were evaluated with output waveform data. I created a config file to for my postlayout VCO+DCC testbench for better comparison with and without layout parasitics.

Postlayout Test Bench of VCO+DCC

After final layout for VCO+DCC block, our postlayout simulations suggest that the effectiveness of the DCC reduced. The duty cycle of the clock is now max 5.62% deviation from 50% without DCC and max 5.31% deviation from 50% with DCC. Clock frequency range also reduced to 15.76-356.83 MHz.

Postlayout Waveform at control voltage of 1.4V (with and without layout parasitics)

Postlayout Simulation Excel Plots

Areas for Improvement:

We can attempt to reduce parasitics by checking if we can reduce length of metal interconnects or polygate during our layout. We have also yet to do buffer sizing with an output capacitor of 1pF for instance at the output of VCO to simulate input capacitance from other blocks that could be connected to the VCO+DCC block. In our project, we have assumed that output capacitor is negligible which is not realistic in real life.

DC-DC Boost Converter

Project Description:

In this project, I worked in a team of 5 members to create a 12V to 24V DC-DC Boost Converter as part of the 30.107 Power Electronics module. Our work in this module won the first runner up SUTD Wurth Electronics Power Electronics Project Award. I contributed in this project by designing the schematics, creating the simulation files, designing and debugging the PCBs.

Skills Covered:

EasyEDA (Schematics, PCB Layout), Multisim (Simulation), PCB debugging (Oscilloscope, Multimeter, Power Supply), Excel Calculations

Project Details:

The boost converter features an operational amplifier (op amp) schmitt trigger and integrator circuit to generate a triangle wave. The triangle wave is then fed with a comparator to convert it to a signal with PWM to control the switching mosfet in the boost converter circuit. An error amplifier with variable resistors were used to tune the duty cycle of the clock signal to vary the output voltage. Furthermore, we also included protection circuits, such as the addition of a softstart circuit and a TVS overvoltage diode. A fan was added to minimise overheating of switching components such as diodes and MOSFETS.

Final Schematics of Boost Converter

Before designing the PCBs, we simulated our circuit on Multisim to check if we can step up the voltage from 12V to 24V when a load is connected to the output.

Multisim Simulation

For the PCB layout, traces were kept as planes for the boost converter to allow high current to flow. Furthermore, test points and annotations were added to help with debugging. The dimension of the PCB is 4.4 cm x 6.4 cm.

Final PCB 3D View

Before deciding on the final PCB design, we created an iteration of the PCB. For this iteration, the focus was on functionality instead of the size of the PCB. Initially, we decided to add use a SMD inductor to save vertical space. However, we could not solder the SMD inductor given the resources available to us. Hence, we had to adapt and we used use the inductor pads for a through-hole inductor instead. Thus, for our final iteration we used through-hole inductors instead.

Top: PCB Iteration 1 Bottom: Modified PCB Iteration 1

We testing the performance of our boost converter using different kind of high voltage LEDs as load. We were able to achieve an efficiency of around 83% for a 24V LED drawing 1A. We were able to achieve an efficiency of around 75% for 23V 2 LED towers drawing 5.8A.

Project Scale Up

Project Description:

This project was created as part of a 4 month long makeathon, Tech For Good 2023. I created this project with 3 other teammates from different instituitions. For our project, we collaborated with the Movement for the Intellectually Disabled of Singapore (MINDS) to create a smart weighing scale with cloud and MQTT lighting feature to help people with intellectual disabilities weigh baking ingredients. I mainly contributed in designing the hardware of the product.

Skills Covered:

EasyEDA (Schematics), Veroboard prototyping, Soldering and debugging, ESP-32, Arduino IDE, Fusion360, 3D Printing, IMovie, Illustrator, Collaboration

Project Details:

At the start of the project, we visited our clients at a MINDS branch to understand what are some of the challenges they are facing. At this MINDS branch, clients are taught to bake cookies to sell as a living. From our site analysis, we found out that most of our clients have limited numeracy which makes counting above 10 and reading scales challenging. Only a handful of clients are able to take part in the premixing process as they are able to comprehend the digits on the weighing scale. Hence, we decided to create a smart weighing scale to simplify weighing for clients so that more of them can be involved in premixing. We were inspired by a weighing scale with LED indicator lamp found at the site.

Site Analysis at MINDS branch

Our smart weighing scale syncs with a google sheet where users can key in recipe ingredients and the desired weight of these ingredients (SETUP state). Instructors at MINDS guiding our clients will then help to select the recipe they need for premixing on our weighing machine (Selection and Confirmation states).The weighing scale will then prompt clients to collect ingredients from the shelf (Light Up Ingredients state). After ingredient collection, clients will return to the weighing scale and the weighing scale will prompts clients to start weighing (Weighing state).

Workflow of Product

To make it more intuitive for our clients, we added a LED ring to prompt clients if current weight exceeds the desired weight of the ingredient keyed in the google sheet so that they do not need to read the weight measurement on the scale. This is paired with audio cues as well.

Indicator LED on Weighing Scale

For this project, I used mainly store bought modules and did veroboard prototyping to connect the modules together. I researched on how each modules work and helped implementing the code to the hardware using Arduino IDE. Below are the schematics I designed and created for our product.

Schematics for Smart Weighing Scale

Schematics for Inventory MQTT Light

In our final prototype, we used the load cell and the plastic base of the store bought weighing scale. The gray portion of the enclosure were designed in Fusion360 and 3D printed as we have to customise the enclosure to accomodate our electronics.

30.007 Project HeaTable

Project Description:

In this project, I worked in a team of 5 to create a stowable heating table that can be added to an aeroplane seat as part of my 30.007 Engineering Design Innovation Module. My group managed to win the SUTD DYSON 30.007 Most Innovative Concept for our work. In this project, I contributed more to the mechanical aspect as I designed the aeroplane seat to be used for contextualisation. I also contributed by doing the base code for the LED ring animations and creating the video animation for our project.

Skills Covered:

Solidworks(CAD, FEA Simulations), Mechanical Prototyping (Wood and Metal Works), Illustrator, Blender, IMovie, Arduino IDE

Project Details:

HeaTable is an aftermarket product designed to enhance the in-flight meal experience. It allows customers to decide when to have their meals, as a heating compartment in the airplane seat table enables them to heat their food on demand. A survey presented at the 2016 IOP Conference (Romli et al., 2016) revealed significant dissatisfaction with existing in-flight meal services: 60% of respondents found the food delivery and waste collection process inefficient, while approximately 50% believed further improvements were needed. Common complaints included dissatisfaction with row-by-row service (48%) and a preference for flexible meal timing (20%). Additionally, a study by Mortensen Ernits et al. (2022) highlighted the increasing demand for customizable meal schedules, reinforcing the need for innovations like HeaTable.

HeaTable Concept Render

To better convey the idea that HeaTable is a stowable airplane table with heating capabilites, I was in charge of creating airplane seats to house our heating tray. I referenced existing airplane seat designs, designed the chair in Solidworks and created the prototype using mainly wood and aluminium profiles.

2024 Chinese New Year Light-Up

Project Description:

In this project, two other group leaders and I led a team of 13 members to create light installations based on the theme of dragons to celebrate along the stretches of road at Chinatown, Singapore. Our team liased with members from Kreta Ayer CC on the design of the light installations and the installations were manufactured overseas. I contributed by facilitating group discussions and designing portions of the light installations. Our work was featured on local media outlets such as the Straits Times.

Skills Covered:

Solidworks(CAD), Blender, Procreate, Teamwork, Leadership, Receptivity towards Criticism

Project Details:

We designed the stretches of the road with families of dragons to convey the idea of reunion and kinship, which are commonly associated with the festival. To help us with ideation, we researched online Chinese paintings of dragons and other auspicious elements.
